Clean Heating Policy Simulator

An AI-assisted interactive website exploring the human cost of coal-to-gas policies in rural Hebei — for villagers, classmates, and policy thinkers.

The Social Issue

Across Baoding, Zhangjiakou, and Shijiazhuang, rural families face a painful trade-off under clean-heating regulations: comply with costly fuel transitions, absorb unsustainable winter expenses, or continue banned heating methods and risk enforcement. Policies such as coal-to-gas (煤改气) often lack tiered, income-sensitive subsidies — leaving low-income households stuck between freezing and breaking the rules.

This project turns that structural dilemma into something people can experience step by step, rather than read about in abstract terms.

Who It Is For

  • Rural residents in Hebei — families navigating real heating costs, subsidies, and compliance pressure.
  • Classmates and peers — students who may never visit a village but need to understand how policy feels on the ground.
  • Policy-minded users — anyone who wants to test how a rule change might affect households at different income levels.

Field interviews in all three cities already confirm the problem exists; this tool lets others walk through it interactively.

Product Type

Not a small game — an interactive policy simulation website.

Users chat with an AI-powered chatbot inside a structured web flow. They choose a role, a city, and household details; the system branches through decisions and outcomes. It borrows the clarity of a choice-based narrative, but the goal is education and research — not entertainment or scoring.

Two roles

  • Rural resident — select a city; enter income, household size, housing area, heating method, usage, and winter cost. The system checks local rules (from scraped policies and regulations), surfaces the household’s dilemma, and offers paths such as continuing non-compliant heating or green transition (natural gas, ground-source heat pump), plus coping strategies (solar, live-stream sales, reduced usage).
  • Policy maker — select a region, draft or paste heating policy text, and see how it interacts with existing local law and affects people at different income levels.
Resident path (simplified):
Role & city → household data → problem analysis → binary choice → transition options & cost forecast → income/coping choices → ending (seized / bankrupt / successful transition) → AI diagnostic summary

How User Feedback Is Collected

Every session is designed to leave a research-ready record while keeping participants anonymous.

  • Pre- and post-simulation surveys — short Likert-scale questions on policy fairness, subsidy awareness, and understanding of rural heating burdens (administered in Chinese for rural users).
  • Behavioral logs — role, city, key choices at each branch, final outcome, and session duration stored without names or contact details.
  • Open-ended prompts — after the simulation: which decision was hardest, whether views changed, and what policy change they would recommend.
  • Informed consent — participants agree that data is confidential and used only for the AIGeneration research paper.

Target sample: 20+ users, split between classmates and rural participants recruited with help from village cadres.

How Feedback Supports the Research Paper

The paper (4,000–5,000 words, English) combines two evidence layers:

  • Primary fieldwork — prior interviews in Baoding, Zhangjiakou, and Shijiazhuang that document the policy–livelihood gap and calibrate simulation parameters (costs, subsidies, compliance rules).
  • Post-use study — quantitative and qualitative data from 20+ people who completed the simulator, showing whether interactive exposure shifts understanding of “one-size-fits-all” clean-heating policy.

Survey scores reveal changes in policy literacy; choice logs show which dilemmas users encounter most (e.g., illegal heating vs. unaffordable transition); open responses and AI diagnostic summaries provide quotable themes for the Discussion section. Together, they answer: Does an AI-assisted simulation make the structural unfairness of current heating policy more visible — and what design lessons follow?
